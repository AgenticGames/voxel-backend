## 1. SANDSTONE (Shallow Layer) — "The Porous Painter"

### Identity
Soft, porous, colorful. Water flows *through* it. The most visually vibrant rock after 1.25Ma of geological processes.

### Process Rankings for Sandstone

| Process | Magnitude | Notes |
|---------|-----------|-------|
| Cementation/iron staining | **UNIQUE, HIGH** | Iron oxide bands, Liesegang patterns — no other rock does this |
| Hydrothermal veins | HIGH | Good fracture host, porous = good fluid pathways |
| Red-bed Cu / supergene | HIGH | Sandstone is a premier red-bed copper host |
| Contact metamorphism | HIGH (near contact) | → Quartzite (NOT granite!) |
| Sulfuric acid | LOW-MODERATE | Dissolves calcite cement only, not quartz grains |
| Karst dissolution | VERY LOW | Pseudokarst via arenisation, not chemical dissolution |
| Silicification | MODERATE | Already SiO₂; manifests as quartz overgrowths cementing pores |
| Flowstone | LOW | Opal/chalcedony speleothems instead of calcite |

### What Heat Produces in Sandstone

| Zone | Distance | Product | Visual |
|------|----------|---------|--------|
| Inner | 0-20m | Massive quartzite + sillimanite | Glassy, grey, conchoidal fracture |
| Middle | 20-80m | Andalusite quartzite | Visible prismatic crystals in hard matrix |
| Outer | 80-150m | Biotite-chlorite quartzite | Green-tinged, partially recrystallized |
| Beyond | 150m+ | Unaltered sandstone | Original tan/orange |

### Metamorphic Minerals in Sandstone Aureole

| Mineral | Formula | Zone | Notes |
|---------|---------|------|-------|
| Quartzite (recrystallized) | SiO₂ | All zones | Pure sandstone product |
| Andalusite | Al₂SiO₅ | Outer (400-600°C) | Prismatic crystals from clay impurities |
| Cordierite | (Mg,Fe)₂Al₄Si₅O₁₈ | Middle | Blue-violet, from Mg-bearing impurities |
| Sillimanite | Al₂SiO₅ | Inner (highest T) | Fibrous/prismatic, near contact |
| Biotite | K(Mg,Fe)₃AlSi₃O₁₀(OH)₂ | Outer | From clay minerals |
| Chlorite | (Mg,Fe)₅Al(AlSi₃O₁₀)(OH)₈ | Lower T | Gives green color to quartzite |
| Wollastonite | CaSiO₃ | High T | Only if calcite cement present |

### Processes UNIQUE to Sandstone

| Process | What It Does | Visual |
|---------|-------------|--------|
| **Liesegang banding** | Rhythmic iron oxide diffusion bands | Red/orange/brown stripes cutting across rock |
| **Moqui marbles** | Spherical iron oxide concretions | Dark balls embedded in/weathering out of walls |
| **Roll-front uranium** | Crescent-shaped U ore at redox front | Dark uraninite at oxidized/reduced boundary |
| **Red-bed copper** | Cu transported through porous red beds | Zoned: hematite → chalcocite → bornite → pyrite |
| **Arenisation** | Cement dissolves, grains loosen | Sandy, crumbly walls; cave by disaggregation not dissolution |
| **Honeycomb/tafoni** | Salt crystallization cavities | Regular pitting pattern on surfaces |
| **Case hardening** | Hard mineral shell over soft interior | Alcoves and overhangs where shell breached |
| **Opal speleothems** | Silica stalactite-like forms | Glassy, translucent white (not calcite!) |

### Ore Deposits Native to Sandstone

| Ore | Mineral | Formula | How It Forms |
|-----|---------|---------|-------------|
| Copper (red-bed) | Chalcocite, bornite, native Cu | Cu₂S, Cu₅FeS₄ | Oxidizing brines through porous red beds |
| Copper (supergene) | Malachite, azurite, chrysocolla | Cu₂(CO₃)(OH)₂ etc. | Weathering of primary sulfides |
| Uranium (roll-front) | Uraninite, coffinite | UO₂ | Redox front in aquifer |
| Iron (cement) | Hematite, goethite | Fe₂O₃, FeOOH | Groundwater precipitation |
| Gold | Native Au in quartz veins | Au | Hydrothermal, near intrusion |
| Turquoise | Turquoise | CuAl₆(PO₄)₄(OH)₈·4H₂O | If phosphate present |

### Sandstone Porosity & Groundwater

- Primary porosity: 15-30%
- Sandstone is an excellent aquifer — water flows THROUGH it (not just over/around it)
- With a nearby intrusion, becomes a convective hydrothermal cell
- Mineral precipitation in pore spaces: quartz overgrowths (reduces porosity from ~30% to ~5%), iron oxide coatings, calcite patches, opal
- Roll-front uranium requires: high permeability + confining layers + redox front

### Visual After 1.25Ma

Red/orange banded walls, sandy textures, honeycomb pitting, green/blue copper stains on fractures, opal crusts instead of stalactites, crumbly zones next to hard quartzite near magma. Water seeps from porous walls (not drips from ceiling). Dramatically colorful compared to all other rocks.

## 2. LIMESTONE (Mid-Shallow Layer) — "The Reactive Star"

### Identity
THE most geologically active rock. Every process hits it hardest. Caves, formations, acid dissolution, skarn — limestone does it all.

### Process Rankings for Limestone

| Process | Magnitude | Notes |
|---------|-----------|-------|
| Karst dissolution | **EXTREME** (10/10) | THE textbook karst rock. 40-150 mm/ky denudation |
| Sulfuric acid speleogenesis | **EXTREME** (10/10) | 10-100x faster than normal karst. Cathedral rooms |
| Speleothem/flowstone | **EXTREME** (10/10) | 300+ formation varieties. Only in carbonate caves |
| Contact metamorphism | VERY HIGH (9/10) | → Marble + spectacular skarn deposits |
| Hydrothermal veins | HIGH (8/10) | MVT deposits, carbonate buffering precipitates metals |
| Cu oxidation | HIGH (7/10) | Carbonate buffering = best malachite/azurite formation |
| Silicification | MODERATE (6/10) | Silica replaces CaCO₃ with chert/quartz nodules |
| Supergene enrichment | MODERATE (6/10) | Works but high pH causes metals to precipitate in oxide zone |

### Sulfuric Acid Speleogenesis Products

| Product | Formula | Amount | Notes |
|---------|---------|--------|-------|
| **Gypsum** | CaSO₄·2H₂O | 70% | 2x volume of dissolved calcite. Chandeliers up to 6m |

| **VOID SPACE** | — | Massive | Cathedral-sized rooms in thousands of years |

### Sulfuric Acid Mechanism (Lechuguilla)

1. H₂S rises from depth through fractures
2. Oxidizes at water table: H₂S + 2O₂ → H₂SO₄
3. Acid attacks limestone: CaCO₃ + H₂SO₄ → CaSO₄ + H₂O + CO₂
4. Gypsum crust forms (2x volume expansion), blisters, collapses
5. Fresh limestone exposed — cycle repeats (self-accelerating)
6. 10-100x faster than carbonic acid dissolution
7. Creates maze caves, cupolas, massive rooms (not single trunk passages)

### Dissolution Rates

| Environment | Rate (mm/ky) | Over 1.25 Ma |
|-------------|-------------|--------------|
| Arid/cold | 5-20 | 6-25 m |
| Temperate | 40-80 | 50-100 m |
| Tropical | 100-150 | 125-188 m |
| Sulfuric acid | 1,000-10,000 | km-scale voids |

### Residues from Dissolution

- **Terra rossa clay**: Red, iron-rich (goethite/hematite) — iconic residual soil
- **Chert nodules**: Survive dissolution, accumulate on cave floors
- **Manganese oxide crusts**: Black coatings (pyrolusite)
- **Pyrite crystals**: Freed from matrix
- **Quartz sand**: Liberated detrital grains

### Speleothem Diversity (300+ types, EXCLUSIVE to limestone)

Stalactites, stalagmites, columns, flowstone, soda straws, helictites, cave pearls, moonmilk, frostwork, coralloids (cave popcorn), cave bacon, rimstone dams, shelfstone, cave rafts, anthodites, draperies, aragonite frostwork

Growth rates:
- Stalactites: 0.005-0.1 mm/yr
- Flowstone: 0.1-1.0 mm/yr (10x faster, turbulent flow)
- Over 1.25Ma: meters of layered deposits

### Speleothem Minerals

|---------|---------|--------|-------|

| Aragonite | CaCO₃ (orthorhombic) | 2-3% | Needle-like crystals |

| Hydromagnesite | Mg₅(CO₃)₄(OH)₂·4H₂O | Rare | Moonmilk/balloons |
| Opal | SiO₂·nH₂O | Rare | Near silica sources |

### Cu Oxidation in Limestone

|---------|---------|--------|-------|

### Processes UNIQUE to Limestone

### Visual After 1.25Ma

Massive cave systems, white/amber stalactite forests, flowstone cascades, red terra rossa clay, dramatic marble+skarn color gradient near magma (white→green→red-brown), gypsum chandeliers in acid zones, vivid malachite green and azurite blue where copper exists. THE most visually complex zone in the game.

## 3. DOLOMITE (Mid Layer) — "The Tough Cousin"

### Identity
Limestone's harder, more porous sibling. Same carbonate family, completely different personality. Slower reactions, unique ores, distinctive textures. Buff/tan/cream color (not grey).

### Process Rankings for Dolomite

| Process | Magnitude | Notes |
|---------|-----------|-------|
| Hydrothermal veins (MVT) | **HIGHEST** — 130% of limestone | THE premier MVT Pb-Zn host rock |
| Contact metamorphism | VERY HIGH (120% of limestone) | → Forsterite marble (richer mineral suite than limestone) |
| Karst dissolution | MODERATE (25-50% of limestone) | 3-4x slower, smaller angular caves |
| Cu oxidation | HIGH (100% of limestone) | Similar carbonate buffering |
| Supergene enrichment | HIGH (110% of limestone) | Better porosity = better fluid flow |
| Flowstone | MODERATE (80% of limestone) | Mg-enriched variants (huntite, hydromagnesite) |
| Sulfuric acid | LOW-MODERATE (30% of limestone) | 2-4x slower, produces epsomite too |
| Silicification | MODERATE (60% of limestone) | Resists more, but produces forsterite when it happens |

### What Heat Produces in Dolomite (COMPLETELY DIFFERENT from Limestone)

| Zone | Product | Visual |
|------|---------|--------|
| Inner | **Forsterite** (Mg-olivine) + **periclase/brucite** | Green crystals, white MgO patches |
| Middle | **Tremolite** + **phlogopite** (golden mica) + diopside | Shimmering gold mica flakes, green |
| Outer | **Talc** + serpentine | Soft, soapy green-white |
| Marble front | **Dolomite marble** (NOT calcite marble) | Cream/buff, harder than limestone marble |

### Magnesian Skarn Minerals (UNIQUE to Dolomite)

| Mineral | Formula | Amount | Visual |
|---------|---------|--------|--------|
| **Forsterite** | Mg₂SiO₄ | 20-30% | THE signature — green olivine crystals |
| **Phlogopite** | KMg₃AlSi₃O₁₀(OH)₂ | 10-15% | Golden mica, visually stunning shimmer |
| Serpentine | Mg₃Si₂O₅(OH)₄ | 10-20% | Retrograde, waxy green |
| Tremolite | Ca₂Mg₅Si₈O₂₂(OH)₂ | 15-20% | White-green amphibole |
| Spinel | MgAl₂O₄ | 2-5% | Small dark crystals |
| Magnetite | Fe₃O₄ | 5-15% | Iron ore (Fe forced out of Mg-silicates) |
| Chondrodite | Mg₅(SiO₄)₂(F,OH)₂ | 2-5% | Humite-group, amber |
| Talc | Mg₃Si₄O₁₀(OH)₂ | 5-10% | Soft, soapy feel |

### MVT Deposits (THE Dolomite Signature — World's Largest Pb-Zn Source)

| Mineral | Formula | Visual | Amount |
|---------|---------|--------|--------|
| **Sphalerite** | ZnS | Honey-brown, banded/botryoidal | 30-40% |
| **Galena** | PbS | Silver-grey cubes | 20-30% |
| **Fluorite** | CaF₂ | Purple/green cubes | 5-15% |
| Barite | BaSO₄ | White tabular blades | 5-10% |
| **Saddle dolomite** | CaMg(CO₃)₂ | Cream curved "pearly" crystals | 10-20% |
| Pyrite/marcasite | FeS₂ | Brassy | 2-5% |

#### MVT Formation Mechanism
1. Basinal brines (3-8x seawater salinity) heated to 50-200°C
2. Brines dissolve Pb and Zn as chloride complexes
3. Separately, H₂S generated by thermochemical sulfate reduction
4. Metal-bearing brine migrates into dolomite
5. Mixing with H₂S precipitates galena + sphalerite
6. Acid byproduct dissolves more dolomite → collapse breccias cemented by ore

### Processes UNIQUE to Dolomite

| Process | What It Does | Visual |
|---------|-------------|--------|
| **Dedolomitization** | Ca-rich water replaces dolomite with calcite | Lighter patches in buff rock, softer to mine |
| **Zebra texture** | Alternating dark/light hydrothermal bands | Striking striped pattern (dark fine + light coarse) |
| **Saddle dolomite** | Curved crystals from hot brines (100-180°C) | White/cream pearly crystals in vugs |
| **Epsomite** | MgSO₄ from acid dissolution (unique byproduct) | White crystalline efflorescence |
| **Enhanced porosity** | Dolomitization creates vugs and holes | Visible holes, "sugary sparkle" from crystal faces |

### Acid Comparison: Dolomite vs Limestone

| | Limestone | Dolomite |
|---|----------|----------|
| Dissolution rate | 100% | 25-33% (3-4x slower) |
| Product from H₂SO₄ | Gypsum only | Gypsum + **epsomite** (Epsom salt) |
| Cave size | Large, smooth, rounded | Smaller, angular, fracture-controlled |
| Cave frequency | Common | Less common |
| Wall texture | Smooth, scalloped | "Sugary" sparkle from crystal faces |

### Visual After 1.25Ma

Buff/tan/cream rock (not grey like limestone). Smaller, more angular caves. Sugary sparkle on walls from tiny crystal faces. Zebra-banded zones near hydrothermal activity. Purple fluorite and silver galena cubes in MVT ore pods. Green forsterite + golden mica shimmer near magma (completely different look from limestone's white marble). More porous/vuggy than limestone.

## 4. SLATE (Mid-Deep Layer) — "The Dark Barrier"

### Identity
Dark, impermeable, foliated. Blocks water, traps ore, splits in sheets. Contact metamorphism is the star — spotted hornfels with chiastolite crosses.

### Process Rankings for Slate

| Process | Magnitude | Notes |
|---------|-----------|-------|
| Contact metamorphism | **DOMINANT** (90% of limestone) | → Spotted slate → Hornfels (NOT marble!) |
| Hydrothermal veins | **HIGH** (110% of limestone) | Bendigo-type gold! World-class deposits |
| Aquitard behavior | HIGH (indirect) | Traps water/ore above — critical for ore concentration |
| Pyrite growth | MODERATE | Pyrite cubes along cleavage planes — visually striking |
| Silicification | MODERATE (50%) | Along fractures, replaces clay minerals |
| Cu oxidation | MODERATE (50%) | Where Cu ores exist in veins |
| Supergene enrichment | MODERATE (80%) | Slate acts as a TRAP layer |
| Sulfuric acid | MINIMAL (2%) | Nearly immune — clay/quartz composition resists acid |
| Karst dissolution | NEGLIGIBLE (<1%) | Insoluble in water |
| Flowstone | MINIMAL (5%) | Only if calcite water drips in from above |

### What Heat Produces in Slate — HORNFELS (NOT Marble!)

Marble comes from limestone. Slate produces hornfels — completely different.

| Zone | Distance | Product | Visual |
|------|----------|---------|--------|
| Outer | Far | **Spotted slate** — cordierite/andalusite spots | Dark grey with lighter 2-10mm spots |
| Middle | — | **Andalusite-cordierite hornfels** + **chiastolite** | Dark brown-black with cross-patterned crystals |
| Inner | Near | **Sillimanite-garnet hornfels** + K-feldspar | Dense, very hard, dark |
| Contact | At intrusion | **Skarn zone** — Cu, Sn, W, Au | Maximum mineral diversity |

### Hornfels Metamorphic Minerals (UNIQUE suite)

| Mineral | Formula | Amount | Visual |
|---------|---------|--------|--------|
| Cordierite | (Mg,Fe)₂Al₄Si₅O₁₈ | 15-25% | Blue-violet spots in dark matrix |
| **Andalusite/Chiastolite** | Al₂SiO₅ | 10-20% | **Cross-shaped carbon inclusions!** |
| Biotite | K(Mg,Fe)₃AlSi₃O₁₀(OH)₂ | 15-25% | Dark velvety sheen |
| Staurolite | Fe₂Al₉O₆(SiO₄)₄(O,OH)₂ | 3-8% | Cruciform twins ("fairy crosses") |
| Garnet (almandine) | Fe₃Al₂(SiO₄)₃ | 5-15% | Red-brown dodecahedra |
| Sillimanite | Al₂SiO₅ | 5-10% | Fibrous, highest grade indicator |
| Graphite | C | 1-5% | From recrystallized organic carbon |

### What is Hornfels?

- Fine-grained, massive, extremely hard and tough contact metamorphic rock
- Conchoidal fracture (unlike slate's flat cleavage splitting)
- Dark brown to black with velvety biotite luster
- Key difference: **hornfels has LOST its foliation** — slaty cleavage destroyed by recrystallization
- Breaks into cubical/blocky fragments instead of flat sheets

### Chiastolite — Visually Stunning

Chiastolite is a variety of andalusite (Al₂SiO₅) containing a distinctive cross-shaped pattern of graphite/carbon inclusions. As the crystal grows in carbonaceous slate, it pushes carbon aside, but impurities accumulate along crystal diagonal planes creating a dark "X" or "+" pattern visible on broken surfaces. Especially common in graphite-rich slate.

### Gold Deposits (Bendigo-Type — World-Class!)

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| Structure | Saddle reef veins in folded slate anticlines |
| Grade | Up to 205 ppm Au (among richest ever mined) |
| Key insight | **Richest gold occurs BELOW slate layers** — impermeability traps ascending gold fluids |
| Total production | ~700,000 kg Au from Bendigo alone |
| Vein fill | 80-95% quartz, 5-20% pyrite, trace-economic gold |
| Associated | Arsenopyrite, stibnite, galena, sphalerite |

### Aquitard Behavior (UNIQUE Gameplay Mechanic)

Slate has extremely low hydraulic conductivity (~0.00019 millidarcy) — effectively an aquiclude.

| Effect | What Happens |
|--------|-------------|
| Water pools ABOVE slate | Perched aquifer — wetter conditions in rock above |
| Dry BELOW slate | No drip-water, no speleothems below |
| Springs at contacts | Water emerges where slate meets cave walls |
| Ore trap | Ascending hydrothermal fluids spread laterally under slate → blanket deposits |
| Supergene trap | Descending metal-bearing water pools on slate surface → concentrated enrichment |

### Unique Visual Features

| Feature | What It Looks Like |
|---------|-------------------|
| Cleavage surfaces | Flat, planar sheets — "stacked cards" appearance |
| Pyrite cubes on cleavage | Bright metallic gold cubes on dark flat surfaces |
| Chiastolite crosses | Dark crosses visible in cut/broken andalusite crystals |
| Spotted slate | Dark rock with regular pale spots (transitional zone) |
| Block falls | Flat tabular slabs (not irregular rubble) |
| Angular caves | Sharp edges, rectangular passages along fractures |

### Caves in Slate

- NOT dissolution — slate is insoluble
- Tectonic/mechanical: faulting, folding, mass movement
- Angular, fracture-controlled passages
- Flat walls (cleavage surfaces), flat ceilings
- No scalloping, no speleothems (unless calcite imported from above)
- Dark blue-grey to black walls
- Floor debris: flat tabular slabs

### Acid in Slate

Nearly immune. Quartz and clay minerals resist sulfuric acid. Only produces surface alteration rinds:
- Illite → Kaolinite (white clay)
- Chlorite → Kaolinite + iron oxides
- Result: white/cream bleached rind on dark slate near acid sources

### Visual After 1.25Ma

Dramatically darker than everything above. Flat, angular walls. No stalactites. Pyrite cubes glinting on dark surfaces. Near magma: spotted texture appears, then visible chiastolite crosses and garnet crystals in dense hornfels. Water seeps at the top contact but dry below. Gold-bearing quartz veins in folded zones.

## 5. GRANITE (Deep/Basement) — "The Endgame Vault"

### Identity
The basement. Nearly indestructible without heat. But with hydrothermal fluids: tin, tungsten, pegmatite crystal rooms. The ultimate endgame reward.

### Process Rankings for Granite

| Process | Magnitude | Notes |
|---------|-----------|-------|
| Hydrothermal veins (greisen) | **HIGHEST** (150% of limestone!) | Greisen Sn/W — EXCLUSIVE to granite |
| Pegmatite crystallization | **HIGH, UNIQUE** | Giant crystal cavities — EXCLUSIVE to granite |
| Supergene enrichment | MODERATE (90%) | Fracture-controlled, where Cu exists |
| Weathering to grüs | LOW-MODERATE | 3-8m penetration in 1.25Ma |
| Cu oxidation | MODERATE (60%) | Where Cu-bearing veins exist |
| Sulfuric acid | VERY LOW (3%) | Attacks feldspars only, quartz immune |
| Karst dissolution | NEGLIGIBLE (<1%) | Silicate dissolution 100x slower than limestone |
| Contact metamorphism | VERY LOW (5%) | Granite IS the heat source, doesn't metamorphose itself |
| Flowstone | NEGLIGIBLE (3%) | Opal-A coatings only, not calcite |

### Greisen Formation (THE Signature Granite Alteration)

1. Granite crystallizes, trapping volatile-rich residual melt
2. Fluids (300-500°C, rich in F, B, Cl) expelled upward
3. Attack feldspar and biotite → convert to muscovite + quartz
4. Feldspar disappears entirely — greisen has NO feldspar
5. Tin and tungsten precipitate as cassiterite and wolframite
6. Topaz, tourmaline, fluorite crystallize as accessories

### Greisen Minerals (EXCLUSIVE to Granite — World's Main Sn/W Source)

| Mineral | Formula | Amount | Visual |
|---------|---------|--------|--------|
| Quartz | SiO₂ | 40-60% | Glassy, vitreous |
| Muscovite | KAl₂(AlSi₃O₁₀)(OH)₂ | 20-35% | Silky silver-white mica |
| **Cassiterite** (TIN) | SnO₂ | 2-5% | Dark brown/black crystals |
| **Wolframite** (TUNGSTEN) | (Fe,Mn)WO₄ | 1-3% | Black tabular crystals |

### Acid Effectiveness in Granite

Nearly useless:
- Attacks feldspars slowly → kaolinite clay (white, powdery)
- Quartz (25-35% of granite) completely immune
- 100x slower than limestone dissolution
- In 1.25Ma: acid penetrates ~6-12m along fractures (vs ~160m in limestone)
- Creates argillic alteration zones: vuggy quartz core → alunite → kaolinite → propylitic

### Granite Caves

- Angular, fracture-controlled passages (not dissolution)
- Three types: fracture caves, blockfield caves, tafoni
- Follow joint sets — rectangular/angular intersections
- No smooth curves, no scalloping
- Thin opal-A/Al-rich speleothems (not calcite)

### Phase-Specific Granite Behavior in Sleep

| Phase | What Happens |
|-------|-------------|
| Phase 1 (10Ka) | Almost nothing. Acid barely touches it. Minor iron oxide staining. |
| Phase 2 (100Ka) | Granite IS the heat source. Slight fracture weathering begins. |
| Phase 3 (500Ka) | **GREISEN FORMS** — Sn/W veins deposit. Pegmatite pockets crystallize. |
| Phase 4 (1.25Ma) | Greisen zones expand. Weathering 3-8m deep. Supergene enrichment. |

### Visual After 1.25Ma

Pink/grey speckled hard rock — slowest mining. Near magma: pale greisen zones with dark cassiterite/wolframite crystals, purple fluorite, black tourmaline sprays. Occasional pegmatite crystal rooms as rare treasure chambers — giant quartz points, emerald-green beryl, pink tourmaline. Angular fracture-controlled passages. Minimal change without heat. **"Heat + granite + time = tin, tungsten, rare gems. Nothing else does this."**
